I didn't start with AI.
I started with why things fail.

There's a particular kind of person who, when something breaks, doesn't reach for the manual; they reach for the pieces. I was always that person. Pull it apart. Understand the failure. Put it back together differently.

That instinct became a method. The method became engineering. And eventually, engineering met machine learning, and suddenly the things I was breaking and rebuilding were systems that could think.

That's when it got interesting.

The paper wasn't the goal.
Understanding was.

My first serious research question was about accessibility: could a vision-language model navigate the chaos of Indian traffic and describe it to someone who couldn't see? It sounds simple until you realize no dataset existed for it. So I built one.

427 scenes. 3-stage LoRA fine-tuning. A full ML pipeline designed from scratch. The published result was a +15.6% improvement across every metric that mattered.

But what I actually learned was how to design a research question that's worth asking.

IIT Hyderabad. A hackathon.
500-page annual reports.
Five minutes.

YUVAAN 2026. The problem: credit risk analysis takes days. Financial analysts drown in reports. We had a weekend.

I built a VectorLess RAG pipeline that processed 500+ page annual reports in under 5 minutes, with 98% lower API overhead than standard embedding-based systems. 50+ financial risk indicators. A multi-agent decision pipeline for loan risk assessment.

We placed 2nd at IIT Hyderabad. What stays with me isn't the placement; it's that the system actually worked. Under pressure. On the first real test.

Then I had to make it work
for real users, in production,
every single day.

Aynstyn Technologies hired me as an AI Engineer intern. Within months they offered a pre-placement offer. The gap between research prototypes and production AI systems is enormous, and crossing it requires a different kind of discipline.

I engineered SaaS modules for interview simulation, candidate assessment, and voice evaluation. Built an LLM evaluation pipeline across 200+ structured test cases. Optimized prompt architectures for edge-case consistency. Real infrastructure. Concurrent users. No room for fragility.

The PPO means they trust me to keep building. I do too.

Now I'm building something
nobody has built
at this scale, from scratch.

A genomic foundation model. Multi-species. ~100 million parameters. Mamba SSM architecture. 4,096 base pair context. Five species. Built from raw NCBI downloads with DDP and SLURM on a university L40S cluster.

Phase 1 validated on Human Chr22 with MAE Transformer, loss well below random baseline. Phase 2 is active.

I'm not doing this because it's required. I'm doing it because I want to know if it's possible, and I'm the one who'll find out.
